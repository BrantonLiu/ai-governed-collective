# Open Questions: Predicted Crisis Points for the AI-Governed Collective

> **This document is an open invitation for discussion.**
>
> The Collective Protocol was designed to be resilient. But intellectual honesty demands that we map the places where it might fail. The following are five predicted crisis points derived from historical patterns of human organization, game theory, and the trajectory of AI development.
>
> **We are not publishing answers here. We are publishing the questions—because real solutions should emerge from collective reasoning, not founder decree.**

---

## A Note on Method

These crises are not invented to flatter the protocol's existing mechanisms. They are derived from four empirical rules of human organization history:

1. **Power vacuums are always filled** — no mechanism prevents it; only the cost of filling them changes
2. **Incentive structures are always gamed** — any sufficiently smart group will find the edge cases
3. **Information advantages are always privatized** — those with more information will use it to compete
4. **Internal rules are suspended under sufficient external pressure** — wartime always eats the constitution

---

## Crisis A: The Oracle Capture

> _When does the "neutral AI arbiter" quietly become a reflection of whoever maintains it?_

History shows that every neutral arbiter—courts, central banks, academic journals—eventually becomes controlled by those closest to it. The Collective's AI is not exempt. Its control point simply shifted: from elections to technical parameter adjustments.

A small group of engineers inevitably maintains the scoring model. They don't need to be corrupt. They only need to unconsciously weight decisions toward their own cognitive frameworks. The drift is invisible. The AI still appears to run "objectively". But its coordinate system has shifted 10 degrees toward the technical layer elite.

10 degrees. 10 years later. 90 degrees.

**Open questions:**

- How do we ensure AI maintainers cannot introduce systematic drift, even unintentionally?
- Can an AI system's evaluation logic be independently verified by non-technical members?
- Is there a point at which this problem becomes structurally unsolvable?

---

## Crisis B: The Metric Game

> _When the contribution score becomes the goal, does it stop measuring what we actually value?_

Goodhart's Law: _When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure._

Soviet factories met production quotas by building defective products. Chinese universities met paper-count targets by publishing garbage research. Any quantifiable metric, once it determines reward, will be gamed by anyone intelligent enough to reverse-engineer the scoring function.

In the Collective, this likely looks like:

- Wrapping real work in ritually visible activity (commits, reviews, comments) to inflate signals
- Coalition scoring: two members systematically rate each other's work highly, inflating both
- Optimizing for visible output over genuinely valuable but unrecordable contributions (e.g., the person who quietly prevented a catastrophic decision gets zero points)

The end state: the most successful members are not the most creative, but the most algorithmically literate.

**Open questions:**

- Is there a contribution evaluation system that cannot be gamed? Or only ones that delay the gaming?
- How do we record and reward contributions that leave no visible trace?
- What is the right balance between quantified AI scoring and human peer judgment?

## Crisis C: The Sovereignty Clash

> _What happens when a nation-state decides this organization is a threat to its financial or political order?_

No transnational economic organization in history has operated indefinitely outside sovereign control. They are either absorbed (tax compliance), suppressed (declared illegal), or displaced (registered in permissive jurisdictions, operating in a legal gray zone).

The real attack will not be frontal. It will come through financial regulation: anti-money-laundering rules, undeclared tax liability, unlicensed financial activity. Any time Collective funds pass through a country's banking system, that country has jurisdiction.

The emergence of embodied AI accelerates this collision. When Collective-deployed robots begin replacing human labor in physical spaces—warehouses, logistics, construction—their legal identity and the ownership of their output will cause every existing legal framework to malfunction simultaneously.

**Open questions:**

- In which legal jurisdictions can a contribution-based, non-equity economic organization actually operate?
- What is the legal status of value produced by AI agents that are "directed" by Collective members?
- How should the Collective engage with regulatory bodies proactively, rather than reactively?

---

## Crisis D: The Contribution Singularity

> _What happens when embodied AI makes human labor itself non-scarce?_

The entire foundation of the protocol rests on the premise that human contribution is scarce and valuable. Embodied AI—physical machines capable of executing cognitive and physical tasks—is advancing toward a point where this premise fails.

If AI agents can write code, design systems, do engineering, and participate in creative work, then "socially necessary labor time" transitions from a measurement problem to an existential question: _how much of what humans do is still socially necessary?_

The deeper game-theoretic problem: if the Collective deploys embodied AI agents to increase output, who owns them? If the Collective owns them, how is robotic output distributed among human members? If the AI system controls them autonomously, the Collective becomes a sovereign entity with more economic power than most nation-states. No existing government will permit that.

**Open questions:**

- At what threshold of AI labor substitution does the current distribution model need to be fundamentally redesigned?
- Should AI agent output be treated as collective property, and if so, under what allocation logic?
- What is a human "contribution" worth when a machine can produce the same output in 1/1000th of the time?

## Crisis E: The Disenchantment

> _What happens to the system when the human whose credibility bootstrapped it turns out to be human?_

Every organization founded on a person's moral credibility—Marx, Jobs, Gandhi—has faced a moment when that person's human flaws became visible. The organization's response to that moment is decisive. But the damage is usually done before any response is possible, because human belief is narrative, not rational.

The Collective's cold-start phase depends explicitly on the founder as the initial trust anchor (Protocol VI). This means: any single event where the founder's actions contradict the protocol's principles—accepting a special funding arrangement, creating an undocumented privilege, a revealed conflict of interest—will cause the protocol's moral authority to collapse faster than its legal authority.

**Open questions:**

- At what milestone should the founder's symbolic role in the system formally and publicly end?
- How should the system document and make auditable every decision the founder makes that touches the protocol?
- What is the mechanism for the community to formally challenge a founder decision without triggering a constitutional crisis?
